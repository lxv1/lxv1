Question. About PID.

If RbR ⊆ aR then a is already a total divisor of b. 

Answer.

An element a in a ring R is a total divisor of b when RbR ⊆ aR∩Ra

Since RbR is an ideal, it is also a right ideal, and because R is a right principal ideal domain, we get RbR = Rx for some x ∈ R. Again, because R is a right PID, and sums of right ideals are right ideals, we have Ra + Rx = Rd for some d ∈ R. Thus, d = r1a + r2x, with r1,r2 ∈ R. Further, x = ar for some r∈R, because, RbR⊆aR and 1∈R.

Now, dr = r1ar + r2xr = r1ar + r2r′x

(for some r′∈ R, because xR = Rx) = r1ar + r2r′ar = (r1a + r2r′a)r ⟹ d = r1a + r2r′a when r ≠ 0, that is when a ≠ 0 (should we have a = 0 the result follows trivially, so let's assume a ≠ 0).

That is, d ∈ Ra, so Rd ⊆ Ra, and Rd = Ra. From our previous equation Ra + Rx = Rd, we see that Rx⊆Ra. But RbR=Rx=xR, so RbR ⊆ Ra. We have, adding the hypothesis, that RbR ⊆ aR and RbR ⊆ Ra, thus - RbR  ⊆ aR ∩ Ra.

Dear Leonid!

About zero-divisors in commutative principal ideal rings:

they usually don't hurt.

Every commutative principal ideal ring is a finite direct sum 

of PIDs and zero-dim local principal ideal rings. 

(Proof is in Brown's "matrices over commutative rings" book. - 

Brown calls zero-dim local principal ideal rings "special PIR".)

We can consider the direct summands separately, because matrix

operations are coordinatewise.


Now let Z(R) be the set of sero-divisors, J(R) the Jacobson radical,

U(R) the set of units of a ring R.


A zero-dim local ring is a ring having exactly one prime ideal;

therefore it obviously satisfies $Z(R)\subseteq J(R)$.


Any ring satisfying $Z(R)\subseteq J(R)$ satisfies 

$Z(R)\subseteq 1 - U(R)$, because J(R)\subseteq 1 - U(R)$.


Let us call a commutative ring satisfying $Z(R)\subseteq 1 - U(R)$

a ring "with harmless zero-divisors".


Every ring with harmless zero-divisors satisfies:


1) if a divides b, and b divides a, say a=bu, then u is a unit.

2) if aR+bR=dR with a=da' and b=db' then a'R+b'R=R 


That's all we usualy need, when we consider PIDs.

Zbl 0657.13019 Anderson, D.D. An existence theorem for non-Euclidean PID’s. (English) 

Commun. Algebra 16, No.6, 1221-1229 (1988). Reviewer: J.Clark MSC:  13F10 13F07 13F15 13H05 

This note presents a simple construction of principal ideal domains which are not Euclidean domains. It is based on the following theorem proved by the author: Let R be an integral domain and let f be a nonzero principal prime in the Jacobson radical of R such that ∩ n=1 ∞ (f n )=0. Then R f , the localization of R at (f), is a UFD if and only if R/Rf is Euclidean. Moreover, in this case R is either a discrete valuation ring or a regular UFD of dimension 2 in which each prime ideal P is generated by height P elements and f does not lie in the square of any maximal ideal. As a consequence of the theorem, it follows that, if R is a two-dimensional UFD with unique maximal ideal M and f is a non-zero principal prime in M, then R f is always a PID but is only Euclidean if f does not lie in M 2 and R is regular local. Conversely, if R is two-dimensional regular local with unique maximal ideal M and f is in M but not M 2 , then R f is Euclidean. It follows that if R is a two-dimensional UFD with unique maximal ideal M, R is not regular, and f is a non-zero principal prime in M then R f is a non-Euclidean PID. For example, one can take R=K[[x,y]], K a field, and f=x 2 +y 3 .

Reviewer: J.Clark

Zbl 0655.13015  Lantz, David C.; Martin, Mary B. Strongly two-generated ideals. (English) 

Commun. Algebra 16, No.9, 1759-1777 (1988). Reviewer: I.Crivei  MSC:  13E15 13C05 

All rings are commutative with unity. By an ideal of a ring R it is understand a fractional ideal, i.e., a submodule of the total quotient ring of R whose product with a single non-zero-divisor is contained in R. An ideal of R is said to be integral if it is contained in R, and it is said to be regular if it contains a non-zero-divisor. An ideal I of a ring R is said to be strongly two-generated if for any nonzero element a of I, there is an element b of I for which I=aR+bR.

Some of the results given by the authors are: (1) For any ring R, the set of regular strongly two-generated ideals of R forms a subgroup of the group of invertible ideals of R: - (2) Let R be a domain of dimension one, with non-zero Jacobson radical J and a non-zero ideal C in common with its integral closure R ' and contained in J. Then the ideal of R[x] generated by C and a polynomial of the form 1+f, where f is an element of xR ' [x] that is nilpotent modulo C[x], is strongly two-generated. (3) Let R be a domain and x and y be indeterminates. Then every strongly two- generated ideals of R[x,y] is principal.
