Zbl 1318.16036 Alahmadi, Adel; Jain, S.K.; Lam, T.Y.; Leroy, A.

Euclidean pairs and quasi-Euclidean rings. (English) 

J. Algebra 406, 154-170 (2014). Alahmadi.pdf

The authors study the interplay between quasi-Euclidean properties and matrix properties (such as diagonalization of rectangular matrices and factorization into products of idempotent matrices) over noncommutative unital rings. Their fundamental concept is that of a right Euclidean pair for a ring R, meaning an ordered pair (a,b)∈R 2 which has a terminating sequence of divisions with remainders of the form a=bq 1 +r 1 , b=r 1 q 2 +r 2 , r 1 =r 2 q 3 +r 3 ,..., r n-1 =r n q n+1 +r n+1 for some q i ,r i ∈R with r n+1 =0. The ring R is called right quasi-Euclidean if all ordered pairs in R 2 are right Euclidean. These properties are related to the possibilities that R is a right K-Hermite ring (for each (a,b)∈R 2 , there exist r∈R and Q∈GL 2 (R) such that (a,b)=(r,0)Q), a GE n ring (meaning that GL n (R) is generated by elementary matrices), or a right Bezout ring (every finitely generated right ideal is principal).

The main results include the following: (I) A ring R is right quasi-Euclidean if and only if it is a GE 2 -ring and a right K-Hermite ring, in which case it is a GE n -ring for all n. (II) An integral domain R is right quasi-Euclidean if and only if R is a GE 2 -ring, every finitely generated projective right R-module is free of unique rank, and every matrix ab00∈M 2 (R) is a product of idempotent matrices. (III) Every matrix ring over a right quasi-Euclidean ring is right quasi-Euclidean. (IV) A left quasi-Euclidean ring is right quasi-Euclidean if and only if it is a right Bezout ring, and a left quasi-Euclidean domain is right quasi-Euclidean if and only if it is a right Ore domain. (V) If R is a right and left quasi-Euclidean domain, then every matrix in M n (R) whose left (or right) annihilator in M n (R) is nonzero is a product of idempotent matrices. This generalizes the result for commutative Euclidean domains due to T. J. Laffey [Linear Multilinear Algebra 14, 309-314 (1983; Zbl 0526.15008)].

The authors also prove that every unit-regular ring is right and left quasi-Euclidean. In contrast, a famous example of G. Bergman appearing in [D. Handelman, J. Algebra 48, 1-16 (1977; Zbl 0363.16009)] is shown to be a right and left quasi-Euclidean, von Neumann regular ring which is not even Dedekind finite (directly finite).

 At the end of the paper under review an example is given, illustrating a particularly simple case of the problem in question. It is as follows. Let C be the field of complex numbers and R the ring of polynomials in the indeterminate x over C with xc=c⎯⎯⎯x for all c∈C. Put a=x2+βi, b=x2−βi with β arbitrary, non-zero and real. As abr=rab for all r∈R, the ideal abR is 2-sided. However, both a and b are irreducible over R. Thus abR is maximal and the simple modules R/aR and R/bR over R are also simple over the simple Artinian ring R/abR, and hence isomorphic. On the other hand, is a∼b, i.e., are there invertible elements u,v∈R such that uav=b? As both u and v must be in C and as cx2=x2c for all c∈C, the answer is no. It follows that R/aR≅R/bR does not imply that a∼b.

   In a wider context, let A and B denote two m×n matrices over a ring R; put A∼B if there are invertible matrices U,V such that UAV=B. Define cokerA=R(m)/A⋅R(n), i.e., the right R-module where R(m),R(n) are columns. If m=n=1, then the above example shows that cokerA≅cokerB does not imply A∼B. The authors state that they have no corresponding example for m=n=2.

   To come to the beginning of the paper: Over which rings R is an arbitrary matrix A equivalent in the above sense to a diagonal one? This old problem is answered here in the affirmative in the case in which R is a ring with identity in which every one-sided ideal is principal. We quote from the paper: "The uniqueness assertion of the `elementary divisor theorem' states that, when R is a commutative PID, cokerA≅cokerB implies A∼B (A∼B implies cokerA≅cokerB for any ring R). Note that the above implication does not require any diagonalizability to make sense.'' However, the authors show that if R is a Dedekind prime ring with right projective cancellation, then cokerA≅cokerB implies that

 Zbl 1291.19003 Magurn, Bruce A. On a note from Oliver concerning generalized Euclidean group rings. (English) Commun. Algebra 42, No. 8, 3350-3365 (2014).

Reviewer: Wilberd van der Kallen (Utrecht). Magurn2.pdf

A ring R is called generalized Euclidean (or GE) if for all n≥2 the group GL n (R) is generated by elementary and diagonal matrices. Now let R=ℤG be the group ring of a finite group G. If GL n (R) is generated by elementary and diagonal matrices for some large n, then the same holds for n≥3 by stability for K 1 . The case of GL 2 (R) is different. The author first treats in detail the elementary abelian 2-group G=C 2 r of rank r. He explains old unpublished work of Oliver, showing that ℤG is GE if r≤3, but not GE for r=4. It is known that stably one has generation by elementary and diagonal matrices for any r. For r=4 there is thus, by surjective stability for K 1 , an element of GL 2 (R) that can be written as a product of elementary and diagonal matrices in GL 3 (R), but not in GL 2 (R). In the final section other finite groups are being considered.

Zbl 0624.20034  Let n be an integer, n≥2. A commutative ring R is a GE n -ring if SL(n,R) is generated by the elementary matrices. For example, combining theorems of P. M. Cohn and A. A. Suslin, the polynomial ring over ℤ in a positive number of indeterminates is a GE n -ring if and only if n≥3·

The author investigates R=ℤ[a 1 ,···,a d ], where the a i are algebraic integers. It follows from standard results of algebraic K-theory that there are only three possibilities, viz. R is GE n for all n≥2; R is not GE n for all n≥2; R is GE n if and only if n≥3. The author produces various sufficient conditions for each of these three possibilities and considers some related questions.

Abstract. Let K be a finite Galois extension of the field of rational numbers with unit rank greater than 3. We prove that the ring of integers of K is a Euclidean domain if and only if it is a principal ideal domain. This was previously known under the assumption of the generalized Riemann hypothesis for Dedekind zeta functions. We now prove this unconditionally.
