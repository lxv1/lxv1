In mathematics, a Bézout domain is a form of a Prüfer domain. It is an integral domain in which the sum of two principal ideals is again a principal ideal. This means that for every pair of elements a Bézout identity holds, and that every finitely generated ideal is principal.

 Any principal ideal domain (PID) is a Bézout domain, but a Bézout domain need not be a Noetherian ring, so it could have non-finitely generated ideals (which obviously excludes being a PID); if so, it is not a unique factorization domain (UFD), but still is a GCD domain. 

The theory of Bézout domains retains many of the properties of PIDs, without requiring the Noetherian property. Bézout domains are named after the French mathematician Étienne Bézout.

In linear algebra, the Frobenius normal form or rational canonical form of a square matrix A with entries in a field F is a canonical form for matrices obtained by conjugation by invertible matrices over F. 

The form reflects a minimal decomposition of the vector space into subspaces that are cyclic for A (i.e., spanned by some vector and its repeated images under A).

 This paper studies presentations of modules over certain rings Λ. First Λ is described. This is a ring which is finitely generated, as a module, over a commutative Noetherian ring R of Krull dimension at most 1. Moreover Λ is an R-order in a semisimple Artinian ring and Λ is contained in a maximal R-order in A. Such a ring Λ is here called a ring-order algebra. Examples of such ring-order algebras include the usual orders over Dedekind domains in central simple algebras, and (with Λ=R) coordinate rings of affine curves.

   The presentations which the paper studies are surjections f:P→U with P a projective Λ-module. Two presentations f, g of U by P are equivalent (f∼g) if there exist automorphisms α, β of P, U, respectively, such that βfα=g. In this language, the elementary divisor theorem says that, for a commutative principal ideal domain Λ=R, any two presentations of U by P are equivalent. One section of this paper leads to a result in this direction when Λ is a noncommutative principal ideal domain; but that result is improved and superseded by a related paper [the authors and C. Furthermore, for certain R (including commutative rings), if A is block diagonal and B is block upper triangular with the same diagonal blocks as A, then coker A = coker B implies A and B are equivalent under a special equivalence. 

This extends results of Roth and Gustafson. As a corollary, a theorem on decomposition of modules is obtained.
