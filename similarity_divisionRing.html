Frobenius normal form wiki

In linear algebra, the Frobenius normal form or rational canonical form of a square matrix A with entries in a field F is a canonical form for matrices obtained by conjugation by invertible matrices over F. The form reflects a minimal decomposition of the vector space into subspaces that are cyclic for A (i.e., spanned by some vector and its repeated images under A).

The author's aim is to find complete invariants for a square matrix A over a skew field K, with respect to similarity (A∼PAP−1), or equivalently to find invariants describing up to isomorphism a K[t]-module V finite-dimensional over K (t a commuting indeterminate, representing a K-endomorphism A of the vector space V).

   The results obtained are correct, important, and interesting; but they do not constitute the almost complete solution to this problem that it is implied that they do. It is shown on pp. 158–159 that if V is ⊕-irreducible, and A algebraic over the center k of K, then A can be written in the form 

[matrix on p.159]

where the Pi are companion-matrices of polynomials pi over K and are mutually similar, and N=ed1 (d= the common degree of the pi). Consider for simplicity the case s=2, d=1. Then P1,P2 are mutually conjugate elements of K, and N=1. One finds that the similarity class of A does not uniquely determine P1 and P2. It determines their conjugacy class, but this conjugacy class is not enough to determine the similarity class of A. Further, though any indecomposable A can be brought to the indicated form, not every matrix of this form is indecomposable. {For s=2, n=1 one easily verifies that A is diagonalizable if and only if the equation P1X−XP2=1 has a solution in K.} Thus, the above reduction cannot be said to give a canonical form for the similarity class of matrices over K.

   Turning to good results, the author obtains, by applying to K[t] the general theory of noncommutative principal ideal domains (developed in his book [Free rings and their relations, Academic Press, London, 1971]), a set of "invariant factors'' for A in this ring, and shows that all but the last of these correspond to matrices algebraic over the center k of K. The transcendental part of the last factor splits off as a direct summand, with transcendental and algebraic summands unique up to similarity. (A matrix A is said to be transcendental if for any polynomial p over k, p(A) is nonsingular.) Little is said about invariants of transcendental matrices over general K, but it is shown with the help of some powerful ring-theoretic machinery that if K is existentially closed (a strong non-commutative generalization of "algebraically closed'') then any two transcendental matrices of the same size are similar. {This points out an important difference between the commutative and noncommutative theories: In the former, the relation of similarity among matrices is invariant under going to the algebraic closure.}

   At the beginning of the article, the author discusses left and right eigenvalues and eigenvectors of A, and gets analogs of some results from the commutative theory, with conjugacy taking the place of equality of eigenvalues.

   {Reviewer's remark: Right [left] eigenvalues correspond to one-dimensional composition factors of V which can be brought to the top [bottom] of a composition series. This suggests that Cohn's "spectrum of A'' be generalized to include not only these two types, but the eigenvalues corresponding to all one-dimensional composition factors.}

Reviewed by G. M. Bergman

   From now on, matrices are over a skew field K which is an algebra over a commutative field k. The matrix A over K is called cyclic with invariant factor λ if xI−A is stably associated to a 1×1 matrix λ. The latter turns out to be uniquely determined up to similarity. The author's form of the Cayley-Hamilton theorem is then: If A is cyclic over K with invariant factor p(x) then p(A)=0.

Inner derivations of division rings and canonical Jordan form of triangular operators. Proc. Amer. Math. Soc. 94 (1985), no. 3, 383–386.

16A39 (15A33)

The author's aim is a uniqueness theorem for the Jordan canonical form over a skew field [cf. the reviewer, Math. Z. 132 (1973), 151–163; MR0325646]. Let F be the matrix with 1's on the superdiagonal and 0 elsewhere; a generalized Jordan block is defined as J(α,β)=αI+βF (for β=1 this reduces to the ordinary Jordan block). Let D be a skew field with centre k and let V be an n-dimensional right D-space. If u is an endomorphism of V which is algebraic over k and (relative to a suitable basis) has a triangular matrix, then u has a matrix which is a diagonal sum of generalized Jordan blocks J(α,β) with β∉[α,D]={αx−xα:x∈D}. Here the α are unique up to conjugacy and β may be chosen arbitrarily subject to the condition β∉[α,D]. If α is separable over k, β may be taken to be 1. The description of β is further clarified by showing that for any λ algebraic over k, but not in k, with centralizer K, one has for any μ∉[λ,D], D=[λ,D]⊕Kμ=[λ,D]⊕μK.

Matrices having strong normal form over a division ring. (Chinese. English, Chinese summary)

Acta Math. Sinica (Chin. Ser.) 51 (2008), no. 2, 371–380. 15A33 (15A21)

Let D be a division ring and A be an n×n matrix over D. Then λI−A can be reduced into a diagonal form diag(1,…,1,φ1,…,φr), where φi is a monic polynomial with φ1∣⋯∣φr, by some elementary operations. 

In general, the form is not unique. If it happens, the matrix A is said to have strong normal form.

   In this paper, the author mainly discusses when matrices over D have strong normal form and obtains some necessary and sufficient conditions for such matrices.

   This paper will be useful for researchers interested in matrix theory over special rings. Reviewed by Yaoming Yu

MR0289666   Stebe, Peter F. Conjugacy separability of groups of integer matrices. Proc. Amer. Math. Soc. 32 1972 1–7. (Reviewer: Morris Newman) 20.75  

Stebe2.pdf integer

An element g of a group G is said to be conjugacy distinguished if any element h of G is either conjugate to g, or there is a homomorphism φ of G onto a finite group such that φ(h) is not conjugate to φ(g). G is said to be conjugacy separable if every one of its elements is conjugacy distinguished. The author proves that SL(n,Z), GL(n,Z) are conjugacy separable if and only if n=1 or 2. He also shows that SL(n,Zp) and GL(n,Zp) are conjugacy separable for all n and all p. Here Z is the ring of integers, Zp the ring of p-adic integers.
