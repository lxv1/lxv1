January 20


Alexander Retakh, MIT

Title:
Structure Theory and Representations of Conformal Algebras
Abstract: Conformal algebras first appeared in attempts to establish a rigorous algebraic formalism of conformal field theory. They are also extremely useful in the study of infinite-dimensional Lie algebras and their representations. I will define conformal algebras, explain their relation to vertex algebras and superconformal algebras of string theory, and describe recent progress and conjectures in the field.


January 27


Arsen Elkin, PSU

Title:

On Hyperelliptic Jacobians with Real Multiplication
Abstract: In his work, Yuri Zarhin provides many conditions on the Galois action on the 2-division points of a hyperelliptic jacobian under which its endomorphism ring is trivial. We consider hyperelliptic jacobians which admit multiplication from an order of a real quadratic field, and show that under some constraints these jacobians have no other endomorphisms over the algebraic closure of the ground field (with some exceptions in positive characteristic).


February 3


Song Heng Chan, UIUC

Title:

On Generalized Lambert series, Ramanujan's $_1\psi_1$ summation formula and Sears' general transformation
Abstract: We use the method of partial fractions to construct two infinite families of generalized Lambert series identities, and deduce several known identities from them. They include an identity due to M. Jackson, a corollary of Ramanujan's $_1\psi_1$ summation formula, and a recent identity of G. E. Andrews, R. P. Lewis, and Z.-G. Liu. Next, we show that the same method also leads to new proofs of Ramanujan's $_1\psi_1$ summation formula and Sears' general transformation of basic hypergeometric series.


February 10


Sun Tae Soh, Myongji Univ.

Title:

A vanishing conjecture for Rademacher's formula for p(n)
Abstract: In 1938 D. H. Lehmer asserted (without proof) that for a prime number p, if A_p(n) = 0, then A_mp(n) = 0 for m = 2, 3, ..., where A_k(n) is the k-th term of Rademacher's formula for the partition function p(n). In our talk, we will discuss a vanishing conjecture, which is more general than D. H. Lehmer's assertion, for the terms of Rademacher's formula for p(n). We discovered our conjecture in 1997 and until last summer we thought that it has been proved by others, but it seems not. This conjecture is very useful in reducing computation time a lot for computing p(n) using Rademacher's formula for it, especially when n is large. Cf. http://trinitas.mju.ac.kr/intro2numbpart.html


February 17


Alexander Borisov, PSU

Title:

Two new connections between algebraic geometry and number theory
Abstract: The talk will be divided equally between two unrelated topics. The first one concerns the surprising connection between some special curves in 3-space and the abc polynomials. It was discovered recently as a by-product of a question of J. Harris. It yielded a nice little problem that was open for several weeks until ultimately solved by N. Elkies. It leads to a natural generalization of the abc polynomial construction that deserves further study. In the second part of the talk I will explain a connection between some families of cyclic quotient singularities and the integer ratios of factorials, through a classical theorem of Landau. I will discuss some known results and open questions arising from this connection.


February 24


Gary Mullen, PSU

Title:

Algebraic constructions for sets of partially orthogonal latin squares and projective planes
Abstract: This is joint work with A.D. Keedwell (University of Surrey) We investigate the construction of sets of latin squares of a given non-prime power order $q$ which are as close as possible to being mutually orthogonal sets. Using uniform cyclic neofields, for any even $q>2$ we are able to construct $q-1$ latin squares of order $q$ with the property that, when we juxtapose any two of them, we obtain $5q-4$ distinct ordered pairs. More precisely, we obtain $4q-3$ ordered pairs that occur exactly once, $q-1$ pairs that occur exactly $q-3$ times, and $q^2-5q+4$ pairs that do not occur. Thus for example in the case when $q=6$ and there is not even a pair of orthogonal latin squares of order 6, we are able to construct 5 latin squares of order 6 so that when any two of them are juxtaposed, we obtain 26 distinct ordered pairs. And when $q=10$, using a uniform cyclic neofield we are able to construct 9 latin squares of order 10 with the property that when any two of them are juxtaposed we obtain 46 distinct ordered pairs. It is conjectured, and very likely true, that $D$-neofields exist for any finite order $q$ except for $q=2,6$. In cases where a $D$-neofield exists, one can do even better. For example when $q=10$, we can construct 9 latin squares of order 10 so that of the total possible $\binom {9} {2} 10^2 =3600$ possible ordered pairs in a complete set of 9 MOLS of order 10, we obtain 2952 distinct ordered pairs. This represents 82\% of the total possible pairs in a complete set.


March 3


Mihai Caragiu, Ohio Northern Univ.

Title:

Some results involving sequences and graphs
Abstract: We will first prove an "intermediate value theorem" with an arithmetic flavor, involving the consecutive averages of a sequence (a result obtained jointly with Laurence D. Robinson). Some other results on sequences will be discussed, including an analysis of a class of 'entangled' Lucas configurations. Finally, a linearized version of a result by Conway and Gordon will be provided. Conway and Gordon proved in 1983 that any spatial embedding of the complete graph with six vertices contains a pair of linked triangles. We will address the case of linear (straight-line) embeddings and we will see that every such embedding has either 1 or 3 pairs of linked triangles.

New Results for Overpartitions
Abstract: An overpartition of the nonnegative integer n is a partition of n wherein the first occurrence of parts of each size may be overlined. Several mathematicians, including S. Corteel, J. March 24


Louise Nyssen, Strasbourg University, France (visiting PSU)

Title:

Test vectors and their relations with L-functions.
Abstract: Let G be a p-adic group and V1, V2, V3 be three admissible irreducible representations of G. Let V be the tensor product of V1, V2, and V3. The space of G-invariant linear forms on V is known to be of dimension at most one. When it is exactly one, finding a vector whose image is not zero is a natural question. Such a vector is called a test vector. Gross and Prasad could find test vectors for certain triples of representations. Inspired by recent papers of Bernstein ans Reznikov, I would like to find some explicit test vectors in order to get estimates of local factors of triple products of automorphic forms. Those triple products are related to the behaviour of some L-function on the critical line.


March 31


Bruce Berndt, UIUC

Title:

Ramanujan's Forty Identities for the Rogers-Ramanujan Functions
Abstract: The Rogers-Ramanujan identities are among the most famous identities in combinatorics. Late in his stay in England, Ramanujan derived 40 further identities relating the two Rogers-Ramanujan functions at different arguments. Although almost all of the forty identities have now been proved, principally by L. J. Rogers, G. N. Watson, D. Bressoud, and A. J. F. Biagioli, an impenetrable fog still lies over the ideas which led Ramanujan to derive these identities. In the past four years, a group of mathematicians of order 8 have been attempting to find proofs of Ramanujan's 40 identities in the spirit of Ramanujan's mathematics. At this moment, we have proofs of thirty-five of the identities that could have been given by Ramanujan. For the remaining five identities, we have found nonrigorous arguments using asymptotic analysis to establish the identities. It is likely that Ramanujan utilized asymptotic methods to discover some, perhaps many, of his identities. In this lecture, we discuss the various methods used to deduce the 40 identities.

Joseph Hundley, PSU

Title:

the Rankin-Selberg method
Abstract: I will discuss some recent joint work with David Ginzburg on the Rankin-Selberg method. Our work relates poles of automorphic L-functions, nonvanishing of period integrals, and functorial lifting. The purpose of this talk will be to give an introduction to the setting and the problems, and to explain why these three things are naturally related, and what the steps are to proving such results.

David Terhune, PSU

Title:

Weight-Depth Theorems for Multiple L-values
Abstract: This is a preliminary report. Borwein and Girgensohn conjectured in 1996 that a multiple zeta value is reducible if the weight and depth have opposite parities. This has been recently proven by Tsumura, by using mainly power series manipulations. My aim is to generalize this theorem to multiple L-values by using the monodromies of multiple polylogarithms associated to Dirichlet characters.

Alf van der Poorten, Macquarie University, Sydney (visitng Brown)

Title:

Somos Sequences
Abstract: In the early nineties Michael Somos asked for the inner meaning of the fact that the quadratic recursions $$C_{h-2}C_{h+2}=C_{h-1}C_{h+1}+C_{h}^2$$ and $$B_{h-2}B_{h+3}=B_{h-1} B_{h+2} + B_{h}B_{h+1}$$ are respectively satisfied by the bi-directional sequences $\ldots\,$, $2$, $1$, $1$, $1$, $1$, $2$, $3$,$7$, $23$, $59$, $\ldots\,$ and $\ldots\,$, 3$, $2$, $1$, $1$, $1$, $1$, $1$, $2$, $3$, $5$, $11$, $37$, $83$, $\ldots\,$, surprisingly consisting entirely of integers [which incidentally, increase at a hefty clip: the log of their $h$-th term is $O(h^2)$ as $h\to\infty$]. In the meantime I had found quite independent reason for studying the continued fraction expansion of square roots of polynomials. Nonetheless, I found that those expansions inter alia produce sequences of integers satisfying quadratic recursions of Somos type. For instance, the sequence $(C_h)$ [called $4$-Somos by some of its friends] reports the denominators of a sequence of points on the quartic curve $Y^2=(X^2-3)^2+4(X-2)$, equivalently of the points $M+hS$, with $M=(-1,1)$, $S=(0,0)$ on the cubic curve $V^2-V=U^3+3U^2+2U$. Indeed all Somos~$4$ and Somos~$5$ sequences are `elliptic sequences' in such a sense. I will tell the story from very first principles and will mention generalisations. Thus, again for instance, the sequence $\ldots\,$, $2$, $1$, $1$, $1$, $1$, $1$, $1$, $2$, $3$, $4$, $8$, $17$, $50$,$\ldots\,$ given by the recursion $B_{h-3}B_{h+3}=B_{h-2}B_{h+2}+B_{h}^2$ arises from adding multiples of the class of the divisor at infinity on the Jacobian of the curve $Y^2=(X^3-4X+1)^2+4(X-2)$ of genus~$2$ to the class of the divisor given by $[(\varphi,0),(\overline\varphi,0)]$; here, no doubt to the joy of adherents to the cult of Fibonacci, $\varphi$ denotes the golden ratio. In calmer words, I detail the continued fraction expansion of the square root of a monic polynomials of even degree. I note that each step of the expansion corresponds to addition of the divisor at infinity, and interpret the data yielded by the general expansion. In the quartic and sextic cases I observe explicitly that the parameters appearing in the continued fraction expansion yield integer sequences defined by bilinear relations instancing sequences of Somos type.
